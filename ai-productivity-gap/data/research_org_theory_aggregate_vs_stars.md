# Research Notes: Organizational Output as "The Integral Under the Curve"

**Research date:** February 2026

## 1. Team Performance Depends on Average/Median Skill, Not Just Stars

**Woolley, Chabris, Pentland, Hashmi & Malone (2010)** -- "Evidence for a Collective Intelligence Factor in the Performance of Human Groups," *Science*.
In two studies with 699 people, teams exhibited a "collective intelligence" factor (c) that predicted group performance across diverse tasks. Critically, c was *not* correlated with the average or maximum individual intelligence of group members. Instead, it correlated with the average social sensitivity of members (r = 0.26) and equality of conversational turn-taking (r = -0.41). **Implication: Group output is driven by distributed interpersonal qualities across the whole team, not by the smartest person in the room.**

**Google's Project Aristotle (2012-2015)** -- Internal study of 180 Google teams.
Google found that "who is on a team matters less than how the team members interact, structure their work, and view their contributions." Psychological safety -- the belief that you will not be punished for making mistakes -- was the single strongest predictor of team performance, innovation, and long-term success. This challenged expectations that individual talent and team composition would be the primary drivers.

**Huckman, Staats & Upton (2009)** -- "Team Familiarity, Role Experience, and Performance: Evidence from Indian Software Services," *Management Science*.
Using detailed project data from an Indian software firm, the researchers found that team familiarity (the average number of times each member has worked with every other member) had a significant positive effect on performance. Team familiarity helped members locate knowledge, share it, and respond to each other. **The implication is that accumulated relational capital across the whole team matters more than importing a single star.**

Sources:
- [Woolley et al. (2010) in Science](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1193147)

## 2. "Weakest Link" Effects in Organizations

**Kremer (1993)** -- "The O-Ring Theory of Economic Development," *Quarterly Journal of Economics*.
Named after the Challenger disaster (where a single faulty O-ring destroyed the shuttle), Kremer models production as a multiplicative function across tasks. If any one worker fails at their task, they destroy a fraction of the final product's value. The key implication: quality enters multiplicatively, so a chain of nine excellent workers and one mediocre one produces far less than ten good workers. **This model predicts that high-skill workers will sort into the same firms, and that the cost of one weak link is devastating in high-value production.**

**Steiner (1972)** -- *Group Process and Productivity* (book).
Steiner's foundational taxonomy classifies group tasks by how individual contributions combine:
- **Conjunctive tasks**: Group output is determined by the *least capable* member (e.g., a mountain climbing team goes only as fast as its slowest climber). This is the "weakest link" archetype.
- **Additive tasks**: Group output is the *sum* of all members' contributions (e.g., tug-of-war). Here, every member matters equally.
- **Disjunctive tasks**: Group output depends on the *best* member (e.g., a quiz bowl where one person can answer). This is the "star" archetype.
His formula: Actual Productivity = Potential Productivity - Process Losses. **Most real-world organizational work is some blend of conjunctive and additive -- meaning the floor and the average matter more than the ceiling.**

**Valcea, Hamdani & Bradley (2019)** -- "Weakest Link Goal Orientations and Team Expertise: Implications for Team Performance," *Small Group Research*.
Examined how the weakest member's characteristics shape team outcomes, finding that in interdependent tasks, the orientation and competence of the lowest performer has outsized influence on overall team effectiveness.

## 3. Performance Distributions in Knowledge Work

**O'Boyle & Aguinis (2012)** -- "The Best and the Rest: Revisiting the Norm of Normality of Individual Performance," *Personnel Psychology*, 65, 79-119.
This is the landmark study. Across five studies with 198 samples and 632,599 individuals (academics, entertainers, politicians, athletes), they found that individual performance is *not* normally distributed. Instead, it follows a Paretian (power law) distribution where a small number of "stars" produce a disproportionate share of output.

**Aguinis & O'Boyle (2014)** -- "Star Performers in Twenty-First Century Organizations," *Personnel Psychology*, 67, 313-350.
The follow-up paper argued that changes in modern knowledge work (greater autonomy, less routine, more leverage through technology) have amplified the power law. A small proportion (10-20%) of employees contributes disproportionately to unit performance.

**What the power law means for aggregate output**: Even under a power-law distribution, the aggregate output of an organization is still the integral under the full curve. The tail is fat, but the body of the distribution (the "rest") is vast. If you have 1,000 engineers and 50 are 10x performers, those 50 contribute enormously -- but the other 950 still produce the majority of total work. **The power law makes stars more visible, but it does not make the middle irrelevant to total output.**

**Lotka's Law (1926) and Price's Law (1963)**
Lotka's law: the number of authors making n contributions is proportional to 1/n^2 of single-publication authors. Price's law: half of all publications in a field come from the square root of total authors (so in a group of 100, ~10 produce half the output). These bibliometric laws describe concentration of output but also reveal that the other half comes from the remaining 90. **Removing the "non-stars" would halve total output.**

## 4. Organizational Productivity and the Full Distribution

**Deming's 94% Rule** -- W. Edwards Deming estimated that 94% (sometimes stated as 85-98%) of problems in a business are created by systems, not by individual workers. His core argument: it is the structure of the organization, not individual talent, that holds the key to improving the quality of output. **If most variation in output is system-driven, then optimizing for a few stars while ignoring the system that the average person works within is fundamentally misguided.**

**Lazear, Shaw & Stanton (2015)** -- "The Value of Bosses," *Journal of Labor Economics*, 33(4).
Using data on technology-based services workers, they found that replacing a boss in the bottom 10% of quality with one in the top 10% increased team output by more than adding a tenth worker to a nine-person team (12% productivity gain vs. ~11%). **This shows that the quality of the person managing/enabling average performers has enormous leverage on aggregate output -- the boss is a multiplier on the full distribution.**

**Bloom (1999)** -- Research on salary dispersion in Major League Baseball.
Greater pay dispersion within a team (measured by Gini coefficient) led to lower individual and team performance. Teams with more compressed wages cooperated better.

**Weinberg (2016)** -- "Talent Recruitment and Firm Performance: The Business of Major League Sports," *Journal of Sports Economics*.
Using Census Bureau confidential data on MLB/NBA/NFL teams, Weinberg found that aggregate team-level worker statistics correlate with win percentage, but there was "no convincing support for the incremental benefit of hiring superstars." **Average talent predicted wins; superstar acquisitions did not reliably translate to profit or performance.**

## 5. Sports Analytics Analogies

**Anderson & Sally (2013)** -- *The Numbers Game: Why Everything You Know About Soccer Is Wrong*.
The most directly relevant finding: soccer is a "weak link" sport. The 8th, 9th, 10th, and 11th best players on a team determine results more than the star. Analyzing 8,000+ matches from 2005-2011 across four top European leagues, they found that improving a team's worst player from 38% to 48% quality is worth 13 goals per season, while improving the best player from 82% to 92% is worth only 10 goals. **Investing in the bottom of the roster yields higher returns than investing in the top.**

**Moneyball / Oakland Athletics (2002)**
The A's fielded the third-lowest payroll in baseball ($40M vs. the Yankees' $126M) yet won 103 games by identifying undervalued metrics (on-base percentage) and assembling roster *depth* rather than buying stars. **This is the "integral under the curve" argument made literal: total on-base events across the full lineup, not the slugging of one MVP.**

**Swaab, Schaerer, Anicich, Ronay & Galinsky (2014)** -- "The Too-Much-Talent Effect," *Psychological Science*, 25(8), 1581-1591.
Across three archival studies of professional sports, they found that too much top talent *hurts* team performance in interdependent sports (basketball, football) but not in independent sports (baseball). In basketball and football, once the ratio of elite to non-elite players exceeded roughly 2:1, coordination broke down -- more talent produced fewer assists, fewer defensive rebounds, and lower field-goal percentages. **In interdependent work (which describes most knowledge work), there is a point where more stars actively harm output.**

Sources:
- [Anderson & Sally - The Numbers Game (Penguin)](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/314351/the-numbers-game-by-chris-anderson-and-david-sally/)
- [Swaab et al. (2014) in Psychological Science](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0956797614537280)
- [The Surprising Problem of Too Much Talent - Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/the-surprising-problem-of-too-much-talent/)

## 6. Counterarguments: When Stars DO Disproportionately Determine Output

**Netflix "Talent Density" Philosophy** -- Netflix's culture deck explicitly embraces the star model: "In creative and inventive work, the best performers are 10x better than their peers." Their strategy is to increase "talent density" by paying top-of-market, applying the "keeper test," and offering generous severance to adequate performers.

**Groysberg (2010)** -- *Chasing Stars: The Myth of Talent and the Portability of Performance*, Princeton University Press.
Boris Groysberg at Harvard studied 1,000+ star Wall Street analysts and found that when stars changed firms, they suffered an immediate and lasting performance decline. Their excellence depended heavily on their former firms' resources, culture, networks, and colleagues. **This is actually a double-edged finding: stars are real, but their performance is partly a product of the system around them. It supports "the integral" argument as much as the "star" argument.**

**Steiner's Disjunctive Tasks** -- Some tasks genuinely depend on the single best contributor (e.g., solving a difficult bug, making a breakthrough discovery, closing a major deal). In these contexts, the star model applies. **The key moderator is task interdependence: in disjunctive tasks, stars dominate; in conjunctive and additive tasks (which constitute most organizational work), the average or the minimum matters more.**

## Summary Table: When Does Each Model Apply?

| Factor | "Star" Model Dominates | "Integral/Aggregate" Model Dominates |
|--------|----------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Task type | Disjunctive (one answer needed) | Conjunctive or additive (all contribute) |
| Interdependence | Low (individual contributions separable) | High (output requires coordination) |
| Production function | Additive (sum of parts) | Multiplicative (quality compounds) |
| Work type | Breakthrough/creative discovery | Execution, delivery, operational |
| Failure mode | Missing insight | Accumulated errors, dropped balls |
| Sports analogy | Baseball (individual at-bats) | Soccer/football (continuous play) |
| Key citation | Aguinis & O'Boyle (2014) | Kremer (1993), Anderson & Sally (2013) |

## Key Takeaway for the Blog Post

1. **Most organizational work is conjunctive or additive**, meaning total output depends on the full distribution of contributors, not just the top.
2. **In multiplicative production (O-ring), the floor matters more than the ceiling** -- one weak link destroys value faster than one star creates it.
3. **Even under a power-law distribution**, where stars produce disproportionately more per capita, the "rest" still produce the majority of total output in aggregate. Removing the non-stars halves output.
4. **Too many stars can actively harm** interdependent teams through coordination failures and status competition (Swaab et al. 2014).
5. **Star performance is partly a system property**, not purely individual (Groysberg 2010, Deming, Google Aristotle).
6. **The strongest counterargument** is in genuinely disjunctive, creative work where one breakthrough matters more than 100 incremental contributions.

The honest synthesis: stars are real and they matter, but *organizational* productivity is determined by the full distribution. Obsessing over 10x performers while neglecting the environment, systems, and capabilities of the other 90% is like optimizing the turbocharger while the engine has a cracked block.
